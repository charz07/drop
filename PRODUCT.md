# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Food-curious consumers (20s–30s) who enjoy discovering new CPG brands but don't know where to look beyond grocery store shelves. They use the app casually — a quick session to see what's new, react to a few brands, check what their taste profile says about them. They are not researchers or analysts; they want the app to do the curation work for them.

Currently: ~10 private beta users, proof-of-concept stage.

## Product Purpose

Drop surfaces 4 emerging food and drink brands per session, matched to the user's taste profile via embeddings. Users react (Like / Maybe / Not for me), and the algorithm learns their preferences over time. The profile page reflects what their reactions say about their taste.

Success looks like: a user finishing a drop and immediately wanting the next one, and trusting that the recommendations are improving with each session.

## Brand Personality

Confident. Curatorial. Understated.

Drop knows what it's doing and doesn't need to explain itself. The experience should feel like getting a recommendation from a friend with very good taste — no disclaimers, no explanation of the algorithm, no hedging. The name says everything: here's your drop.

Emotional goals: delight at discovery, trust in the curation, low friction throughout.

## Anti-references

- Yelp / Google Reviews: noisy, democratic, quantity over curation
- NielsenIQ / market research tools: clinical, data-heavy, survey-like
- Generic food delivery apps (DoorDash, Uber Eats): transactional, no personality
- BuzzFeed food content: poppy, listicle energy, low signal-to-noise

## Design Principles

1. **Trust earns itself** — the UI should never over-explain. If the recommendation is good, it speaks for itself. No match scores, no algorithm explanations.
2. **Friction is the enemy of discovery** — every extra tap is a brand that doesn't get seen. Minimize steps between arriving and reacting.
3. **Signal over noise** — the drop of 4 is a deliberate constraint. Less is more. The scarcity is the product.
4. **The profile is a mirror** — the taste profile page should feel like insight, not a report. What did your reactions say about you?
5. **Dark and considered** — the dark UI is a commitment, not a default. It signals that this is a curated experience, not a utility.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No specific WCAG requirements stated. Target WCAG AA as a floor for contrast. Primary interaction is tap/click on mobile and desktop. No known motor or cognitive accommodation requirements at this stage.
